Project 4: Ethics

Examining The Ethicality of Data in the COMPAS Algorithm
Author

Josh Goldhaber

Introduction

Northpointe’s COMPAS algorithm, implemented across courts of the United States with the intent of more accurately predicting recidivism rates, scores defendants with a “risk rating”. These scores range from one to ten, with one being unlikely to reoffend, and ten being highly likely to reoffend. The scores are based on 137 questions either answered by defendants or pulled from records. Notably, race is not one of those questions. However, there are concerns about the algorithm’s fairness, particularly surrounding race. ProPublica, a nonprofit investigative journalism organization, conducted a study, finding that the algorithm incorrectly flagged Black defendants as future criminals at nearly twice the rate of white defendants, while white defendants were more frequently mislabeled as low risk compared to Black defendants. Its fairness among women and men is also questioned: women at a risk rating of six are just as likely to reoffend as a man at a risk rating of 4.

This scenario illustrates a major ethical dilemma at the intersection of data science and the criminal justice system. COMPAS is intended to bring statistical rigor and reduce human bias in judicial decisions, but the ProPublica findings suggest that the model may unintentionally reproduce inequalities that are present in the data used to train it. Since the algorithm is proprietary and not fully transparent, neither defendants nor judges can clearly understand how risk scores are produced or whether they treat different groups equitably. The central ethical issue is whether it is appropriate or fair to use a predictive model with limited transparency to influence high stakes decisions such as bail, sentencing, and parole, especially when errors appear to fall disproportionately on certain racial and demographic groups.

Question Response

What is the permission structure for using the data? Was it followed?

In the COMPAS example, there are two layers of permission and consent to consider. Courts in places like Broward County adopted COMPAS as part of their routine decision making for bail and sentencing, so defendants did not choose to have a proprietary risk score used to influence their punishment (Angwin et al., 2016). The risk score simply became part of the standard judicial process. Defendants also did not meaningfully consent to having their data used in later fairness audits. ProPublica obtained records through public-records requests, which is legally permitted but does not reflect informed consent from the people whose information was analyzed (Angwin et al., 2016). There is also a broader question raised by scholars: can people meaningfully consent to predictive uses of their data that do not even exist yet? As Andrew Lee Park notes, predictive systems in sentencing often rely on information that defendants never agreed to provide for this purpose, creating “a fundamental mismatch between the collection of data and its downstream uses” (Park, 2019).

Defendants also did not give specific consent for their data to be used in subsequent research audits of the algorithm. ProPublica obtained the data via public records, which is legally permissible, but nonetheless different from participants being clearly informed in advance that their information would be used to probe racial bias in a risk tool and then shared publicly as a dataset. In that sense, informed consent, especially for unforeseen downstream uses of the data, was very limited.

How were the variables collected? Were they accurately recorded? Is there any missing data?

The variables used in the Northpointe COMPAS algorithm dataset were collected through a combination of defendant questionnaires and information pulled from criminal justice records. The COMPAS tool uses 137 items that include factors such as prior arrests, family history with incarceration, school disciplinary issues, employment status, and peer associations, and these responses are combined with demographic information like age and gender (Angwin et al., 2016). Because defendants provide some of this information during the stressful and coercive environment of pretrial processing, there is a possibility that certain responses may be incomplete or inaccurate. ProPublica’s reporting also notes that Northpointe would not disclose exactly how all variables were weighted or whether the company performed internal validation of the completeness of the data it collected (Angwin et al., 2016). Scholars have raised concerns that variables taken from justice system records can embed existing racial and socioeconomic inequalities, and that many questionnaires rely on self-reported information that may be inconsistently recorded (Park, 2019). While ProPublica used two years of follow-up data to identify whether individuals were charged with new crimes, any cases with unclear or unavailable outcomes would be treated as missing, which introduces further uncertainty regarding data accuracy and completeness.

Who was measured? Are those individuals representative of the people to whom we’d like to generalize / apply the algorithm? Should we analyze data if we do not know how the data were collected?

The population examined in the ProPublica analysis of COMPAS consisted of more than 7,000 individuals who were arrested in Broward County, Florida, in 2013 and 2014 and whose cases moved through that county’s criminal justice system (Angwin et al., 2016). Importantly, this dataset reflects only the people included in ProPublica’s study—not the full national population of defendants evaluated by the COMPAS algorithm. Because the ProPublica dataset is drawn from a single county, it is a narrow and highly specific sample shaped by the policing patterns, demographics, and local judicial practices of Broward County, rather than a representative sample of everyone who receives COMPAS scores across the United States.

While COMPAS is used in many jurisdictions with different crime patterns, populations, and institutional conditions, the ProPublica dataset is local and historically contingent, making it an imperfect proxy for evaluating the tool’s broader performance (Park, 2019). Park notes that algorithms or assessments evaluated using such constrained datasets may behave unpredictably when generalized beyond the context in which the data were collected.

This raises a methodological concern: to what extent can fairness claims be inferred from a dataset when the underlying data-generation process is unclear or context-dependent? If we do not fully understand the policing behaviors, arrest practices, or conditions under which defendants completed survey items in Broward County, then any conclusions drawn from the ProPublica dataset—including claims about COMPAS fairness—become more limited and less reliable (Angwin et al., 2016).

Should race be used as a variable? Is it a proxy for something else (e.g., amount of melanin in the skin, stress of navigating microaggressions, zip-code, etc.)? What about gender?

Race is not explicitly used as a variable in the COMPAS questionnaire, but ProPublica’s investigation shows that the algorithm still produces outcomes that differ sharply by race, which strongly suggests that other inputs act as proxies (Angwin et al., 2016). Variables such as prior arrests, neighborhood stability, family incarceration, and school discipline history are all deeply shaped by structural racism and patterns of policing, so including them can indirectly encode racial disparities even without asking about race directly. Scholars at UCLA Law argue that many of the inputs used in predictive risk tools function as “functional proxies for race” because they reflect unequal social conditions rather than individual behavior (Park, 2019). This means that excluding race does not prevent racial bias, and including race would raise its own ethical problems because it would allow courts to make decisions partly on the basis of a protected characteristic. Gender also plays an important role as touched on in my introduction to the algorithm. ProPublica found that women and men with the same risk score do not have the same likelihood of reoffending, which suggests inconsistent calibration across gender groups (Angwin et al., 2016). The core concern is that both race and gender can become encoded in the model through the correlations present in the data, so even when they are not included explicitly, the algorithm may still learn patterns that reflect social inequities rather than genuine risk.

In my opinion, I believe that first of all, there should not be an algorithm that determines your chance of recidivism at all, and most definitely not an algorithm that has any say in your sentencing.

Here is an excerpt from my paper: “Examining the Permissibility of Statistical Discrimination in COMPAS-Based Sentencing” I wrote for my Data Science Ethics course taught by Bradley McHose, expanding on this viewpoint.

The use of an algorithm in general, as a replacement of a judge is impermissible because each defendant should be tried based on the entirety of the facts and information relevant to their case instead of just statistical correlation. To exemplify this argument, I present a case in which the use of COMPAS removed the influence of the court. Paul Zilly, sentenced on February 15, 2023, was convicted of stealing tools and a lawn mower. During the sentencing hearing, the prosecution advised the judge to sentence Zilly to one year in county jail and post-incarceration supervision. The judge however, citing a high risk rating produced by Northpointe’s algorithm, refuted the advised punishment and imposed two years in state prison with three years of supervision. The judge’s choice to rule almost exclusively based on the algorithm removed the effect of qualitative elements about Zilly that would have been considered if the algorithm had not been used. For example, the algorithm recognized that Zilly had struggled with drug usage in the past, correlating that with high likelihood to reoffend, however neglected all of the changes that Zilly has made to stop using drugs and better his life. Its usage of statistical discrimination in this case and others might cause many to see this as grounds for its impermissibility, as it removes the humanitarian aspect of the legal process and devalues the qualitative analysis conducted by lawyers at trial.

Not only does this render the judge a ‘rubber stamp’, it undermines the legal system in its essence: redirecting its function from a system that aims to rehabilitate defendants into a machine that determines sentencing based on their likelihood of committing a future crime.

Aside from the ethics of the use of an algorithm in the first place, the incorporation of race or gender into the algorithm should not be allowed, for many reasons. The most prominent reason of them all is the confounding effect. Because the results of the algorithm are determined by questions related to ancestral or familial jail time and previous sentences, the algorithm can have a snowball effect, where historical patterns of over-policing and structural discrimination become encoded as indicators of individual risk. This means that people from communities that have faced disproportionate surveillance or harsher charging practices may receive higher risk scores simply because the model treats the consequences of past injustice as predictive signals. As scholars note, many variables in risk assessments function as indirect stand-ins for race, which allows the model to reproduce racial disparities even when race is not explicitly included (Park, 2019). ProPublica shows that this dynamic leads to Black defendants being falsely labeled as high risk at nearly twice the rate of white defendants, which demonstrates how quickly seemingly neutral inputs can magnify unequal outcomes (Angwin et al., 2016). By allowing attributes tied to race or gender to influence predictions, the algorithm reinforces existing inequalities and turns social disadvantage into justification for harsher legal decisions.

Were the data made publicly available? Why? How? On what platform?

A central issue highlighted in the COMPAS investigation is that Northpointe never disclosed how the algorithm actually generates its risk scores. ProPublica found that the company would not reveal the formula or the weight placed on any of the 137 variables, calling the model proprietary and declining to share the scoring method with the public or with the courts that rely on it (Angwin et al., 2016). Because of this secrecy, neither defendants nor judges can evaluate how the algorithm reaches its conclusions or whether the inputs are combined in a fair or statistically sound way. UCLA Law notes that this lack of transparency turns COMPAS into a “Black box” that shapes sentencing and bail decisions while hiding its internal logic from the very people it affects (Park, 2019). With no access to the method, it becomes impossible to determine whether the racial disparities observed in the outcomes stem from biased data, flawed modeling choices, or assumptions built into the algorithm. This closed design limits accountability and makes it difficult to challenge or correct errors in a system that directly influences a person’s freedom.

In terms of ProPublica’s research, they made the COMPAS dataset publicly available so that outside researchers, journalists, and policymakers could independently examine whether the algorithm was producing racially unequal outcomes. ProPublica obtained the original records through public records requests to Broward County and then released a cleaned and documented version of the dataset on its website, along with a detailed methodology explaining how the analysis was conducted. The choice to share the data publicly reflects a belief that systems influencing sentencing and bail decisions should be open to public scrutiny, especially when they are known to affect vulnerable groups.

Conclusion (Question 4)

The issues described above matter because they shape who benefits from the use of COMPAS and who is placed at greater risk of harm. When an algorithm is built on data that may be inaccurately collected, drawn from a narrow and unrepresentative group, and influenced by variables that operate as proxies for race or gender, the resulting predictions do not distribute error evenly. ProPublica’s findings show that the burden of these errors falls most heavily on Black defendants, who were far more likely to be incorrectly labeled as high risk despite not reoffending (Angwin et al., 2016). Those who benefit are typically the institutions that gain efficiency or maintain authority by relying on a proprietary system that appears objective on the surface. UCLA Law argues that this reliance reinforces existing power structures because the algorithm’s design remains hidden, which prevents meaningful public oversight and allows court systems to treat its outputs as neutral science even when they reflect structural inequalities (Park, 2019). The neglect is experienced by defendants who are scored by a tool they did not meaningfully consent to, trained on data they did not control, and applied using a method they are not permitted to examine. The ethical violations involved are not simply accidental but serve institutional interests in efficiency, control, and risk management. The secrecy surrounding the model protects the company’s proprietary claims, and the opaque nature of the system strengthens the power of courts and corrections agencies by allowing them to justify decisions using an unchallengeable algorithm. The result is a system in which fairness is secondary to administrative convenience, and the people most affected by the tool have the least ability to contest it.
