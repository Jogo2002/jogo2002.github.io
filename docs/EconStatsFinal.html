Research question

Research question:
Using Cook County records where the case has a current sentence and we focus on the primary charge, how well can we predict sentence length (in years) from judge, race, gender, and legally-relevant controls (offense category, charge class, sentence type, charge count, age, and case length)? After accounting for those controls, do race and gender still show meaningful associations with sentence length?

1(b) Describing the data

https://datacatalog.cookcountyil.gov/Legal-Judicial/Sentencing/tg8v-tm6u/about_data

The data used in this project comes from the Cook County Sentencing dataset, published on the Cook County Open Data Portal and maintained by the Cook County State’s Attorney’s Office (CCSAO), in fact, the office in which my Mother, Catherine Goldhaber used to work in. Cook is a large county in Illinois that covers the Chicagoland area and all of its surrounding suburbs. The dataset consists of administrative court records documenting sentences imposed following convictions in Cook County, Illinois. Each observation corresponds to a single charged offense that received a sentence, rather than a unique individual, meaning defendants with multiple sentenced charges may appear multiple times in the data.

The data are collected as part of the county’s routine judicial and prosecutorial record-keeping process, and republished quarterly. Information is recorded during the normal processing of criminal cases and then released publicly to promote transparency in the criminal justice system.

The dataset is not randomly sampled. Instead, it represents a population dataset of sentencing events recorded by Cook County courts over the covered time period. As a result, inference from this dataset should be interpreted as descriptive of Cook County sentencing practices rather than as estimates from a random sample.

There are several important potential sources of bias. First, the dataset includes only cases that resulted in a sentence, excluding arrests that did not lead to conviction, cases dismissed, or cases resolved through diversion programs. This creates selection bias, as the data reflect later stages of the criminal justice process rather than initial charging or policing decisions. Second, key determinants of sentencing—such as prior criminal history, plea bargaining details, sentencing guideline ranges, quality of legal representation, and evidentiary strength—are not fully observed, raising concerns about omitted variable bias in regression analysis. Third, demographic variables such as race and gender may be subject to misclassification or missingness, as they are administratively.

In terms of representativeness, the dataset is representative of sentenced cases in Cook County, but it is not representative of all criminal defendants, all arrests, or sentencing practices outside Cook County. Because Cook County is a large and diverse urban jurisdiction, patterns observed here may differ substantially from those in suburban, rural, or out-of-state courts. Consequently, conclusions from this analysis should be interpreted as applying specifically to Cook County sentencing outcomes, rather than to the U.S. criminal justice system as a whole.

# Q1(c): Clean data and create variables (including outcome)
# Outcome = years_sentenced
 

sent_clean <- sent |>
  # your intended analytic sample
  filter(current_sentence_flag == TRUE, primary_charge_flag == TRUE) |>
  mutate(
    # numeric coercions
    charge_count = suppressWarnings(as.numeric(charge_count)),
    length_of_case_in_days = suppressWarnings(as.numeric(length_of_case_in_days)),
    age_at_incident = suppressWarnings(as.numeric(age_at_incident)),

    # parse commitment term into a numeric when possible
    commitment_term_num = readr::parse_number(as.character(commitment_term)),

    # Convert commitment to DAYS (keep only true time units)
    commitment_days = case_when(
      commitment_unit == "Hours"   ~ commitment_term_num / 24,
      commitment_unit == "Days"    ~ commitment_term_num,
      commitment_unit == "Weeks"   ~ commitment_term_num * 7,
      commitment_unit == "Months"  ~ commitment_term_num * 30.44,
      commitment_unit == "Year(s)" ~ commitment_term_num * 365,
      TRUE                         ~ NA_real_
    ),

    # outcome in years
    years_sentenced = commitment_days / 365,

    # Clean race (regex approach; keep Missing/Other buckets)
    race_clean = case_when(
      is.na(race) ~ "Missing",
      str_detect(race, regex("^black$", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "Black",
      str_detect(race, regex("^white\\b", ignore_case = TRUE)) &
        str_detect(race, regex("hispanic|latino", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "White (Hispanic/Latino)",
      str_detect(race, regex("^white$", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "White",
      str_detect(race, regex("hispanic|latino", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "Hispanic/Latino",
      str_detect(race, regex("asian", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "Asian",
      str_detect(race, regex("american\\s*indian", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "American Indian",
      str_detect(race, regex("unknown", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "Missing",
      TRUE ~ "Other/Unknown"
    ),

    # Clean gender
    gender_clean = case_when(
      is.na(gender) ~ "Missing",
      str_detect(gender, regex("^male$", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "Male",
      str_detect(gender, regex("^female$", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "Female",
      str_detect(gender, regex("unknown", ignore_case = TRUE)) ~ "Other/Unknown",
      TRUE ~ "Other/Unknown"
    ),

    # Judge: many categories -> lump to avoid an enormous model
    judge_clean = fct_explicit_na(factor(sentence_judge), na_level = "Missing"),
    judge_group = fct_lump_n(judge_clean, n = 25, other_level = "Other judges"),

    # Controls you already started using (lump levels to keep model stable)
    offense_group = fct_lump_n(factor(updated_offense_category), n = 15, other_level = "Other offenses"),
    disp_class_group = fct_lump_n(factor(disposition_charged_class), n = 10, other_level = "Other/Unknown"),
    sentence_type = fct_explicit_na(factor(sentence_type), na_level = "Missing"),

    # Time: sentencing year can matter (policy/guideline changes over time)
    sentence_date_parsed = parse_date_time(as.character(sentence_date), orders = "Y b d I:M:S p", tz = "UTC"),
    sentencing_year = factor(year(sentence_date_parsed)),
    sentencing_year_num = year(sentence_date_parsed),

    # Modeling convenience: log transform for skew
    log_years = log1p(years_sentenced)
  ) |>
  # keep rows where key analysis variables exist
  filter(
    !is.na(years_sentenced),
    years_sentenced >= 0,
    years_sentenced <= 200,              # trims extreme likely entry issues; still allows very long sentences
    !is.na(age_at_incident),
    !is.na(charge_count),
    !is.na(length_of_case_in_days),
    !is.na(offense_group),
    !is.na(disp_class_group),
    !is.na(sentence_type)
  ) |>
  mutate(
    # reference categories for interpretability
    race_clean = fct_relevel(factor(race_clean), "White"),
    gender_clean = fct_relevel(factor(gender_clean), "Male"),
    sentence_type = fct_relevel(sentence_type, "Probation")
  )

Q1(d) Interpretation:
The two-sample t-test comparing mean log sentence length between Black and White defendants shows a highly statistically significant difference (t = −18.18, p < 2.2e−16). The null hypothesis of equal means is therefore decisively rejected. The estimated 95% confidence interval for the difference in mean log sentence length (White − Black) is [−0.065, −0.052], which does not include zero.

The sample means indicate that Black defendants have a higher average log sentence length (mean = 1.196) than White defendants (mean = 1.138). This unadjusted difference suggests a meaningful disparity in sentencing outcomes by race in the raw data.

While this result is purely descriptive and not prepared to extraploate, it provides strong motivation for a more careful modeling approach that controls for legally relevant factors such as offense type, charge class, sentence type, and judge. The presence of a large and statistically precise difference at this preliminary stage supports the validity of the research question and suggests that race, alongside judicial assignment and case characteristics, may play an important role in predicting sentence length.

2. Hypothesis

2(a) Hypothesis and expected trend

Null (H0):
After accounting for legally relevant case characteristics and the inclusion of judge identity as a predictor, sentence length is fully explained by observable case factors, and defendant race and gender have no additional association with years sentenced.

Hypothesis (H1):
Even after accounting for legally relevant case characteristics and including judge identity as a predictor, sentence length is not fully explained by case factors alone, and defendant race and gender remain systematically associated with years sentenced.

Expected trends (why):
- Judge identity is incorporated by including judge-group indicator variables, where the most frequent judges are kept as their own categories and low-volume judges are pooled into an “Other judges” group to keep the model stable and interpretable.

  • Race/gender differences could appear due to a mix of case selection into categories not fully observed (priors, plea decisions, guideline departures) and/or differential treatment—this project is descriptive/predictive, not causal.

2(b) Model Specification, why this trend?

I model sentence length in years on a log scale:

\[ \log(1+\text{years}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1(\text{race}) + \beta_2(\text{gender}) + \beta_3(\text{judge}) + \beta X + \varepsilon \] – This formula visualization is generated by AI.

The primary explanatory variables of interest are judge identity, defendant race, and defendant gender, as these factors capture both institutional discretion and demographic characteristics that prior research and public discussion suggest may be associated with sentencing outcomes. The model also includes legally relevant control variables—offense category, charged offense class, sentence type, number of charges, defendant age at the incident, and length of the case—which are expected to systematically influence sentence severity based on statutory guidelines and case complexity. In some specifications, year fixed effects are included to account for changes in sentencing practices over time due to policy shifts, judicial turnover, or evolving prosecutorial norms.

This functional form is chosen for two main reasons. First, sentence length is highly right-skewed, with a small number of extremely long sentences. Taking the logarithm reduces the influence of these extreme values and helps stabilize the variance of the error term, improving model fit and interpretability. Second, the log specification allows coefficients to be interpreted as approximate percentage differences in sentence length, which is intuitive when comparing outcomes across demographic groups or judges, particularly when baseline sentence lengths vary substantially across cases.

The inclusion of judge fixed effects is motivated by descriptive evidence of substantial variation in average sentence length across judges, suggesting that judicial assignment may meaningfully shape sentencing outcomes beyond case characteristics alone. This variation reflects differences in judicial philosophy, experience, and sentencing discretion, making judge identity a central predictor in a model of sentence length. The inclusion of demographic variables such as race and gender allows the model to test whether observable differences in sentencing persist after conditioning on legally relevant case factors. Overall, the model is designed to capture systematic trends in sentencing outcomes while separating the roles of institutional discretion, case characteristics, and defendant demographics.

2(c) What would make you reject/respecify?

I would respecify if: - diagnostics show extreme heteroskedasticity or nonlinearity even on the log scale, - judge categories are too sparse (overfitting); I address this by lumping judges, - out-of-sample error is poor, suggesting missing key predictors or that a different functional form (e.g., Gamma regression) is needed.

# function to compute performance on original YEARS scale
eval_on_years <- function(model, data_df) {
  pred_log <- predict(model, newdata = data_df)
  pred_years <- pmax(0, expm1(pred_log))  # back-transform; enforce nonnegative
  tibble(
    rmse_years = rmse(data_df$years_sentenced, pred_years),
    mae_years  = mae(data_df$years_sentenced, pred_years)
  )
}

perf <- bind_rows(
  tibble(model = "m0: mean only",      eval_on_years(m0, sent_clean)),
  tibble(model = "m1: demo + age",     eval_on_years(m1, sent_clean)),
  tibble(model = "m2: + case controls",eval_on_years(m2, sent_clean)),

3(b) Evaluate

3(b)(i) Which model performs best? Why?

Model 4, which includes legally relevant case characteristics, judge identity, and a time trend, performs best according to all evaluation metrics. It achieves the lowest RMSE (2.52 years) and lowest MAE (1.05 years) among the candidate models, indicating the most accurate predictions of sentence length on average. Model 4 also has the highest adjusted R-square (0.631), explaining slightly more variation in sentence length than models that exclude judge or time controls. Although the performance gains over Model 3 are modest, they suggest that accounting for both judicial heterogeneity and temporal variation provides incremental improvements in predictive performance beyond case characteristics alone.

Model 4 performs best primarily because it includes the most information, incorporating legally relevant case characteristics, judge identity, and a time trend. By construction, adding additional predictors to a linear regression model cannot decrease R-sqare and will typically improve in-sample fit, even if the added variables have limited substantive importance. For this reason, the improvement in R-square alone should not be interpreted as strong evidence that the added variables are crucial drivers of sentencing outcomes.

3(b)(ii) Do you trust your model? Why?

I trust the model more as a predictive and descriptive tool than as a causal explanation of sentencing outcomes. It performs well in terms of fit and prediction accuracy, explaining about 63 percent of the variation in log sentence length, and the stability of coefficients across specifications suggests that the results are not driven by simple omitted variable bias from offense type alone. However, several important determinants of sentencing, such as prior criminal history, plea bargaining, sentencing guideline ranges, and quality of legal representation, are not observed in the data and cannot be fully captured by categorical controls. As a result, the estimated race and gender coefficients should be interpreted as conditional associations rather than causal effects.

3(b)(iii) Publication-ready? What would you need?

Not yet. To get closer to publication quality, the analysis would need to incorporate additional key controls such as criminal history, plea versus trial status, and sentencing guideline ranges. It would also benefit from robustness checks using alternative samples, such as restricting to felony cases or specific offense categories. In addition, stronger identification strategies, including approaches that rely on plausibly random judge assignment, would be necessary to support causal interpretation. Finally, fairness focused evaluations, such as comparing prediction errors and calibration across demographic groups, would help assess whether the model performs equitably across populations.

Residual Analysis for Zero Conditional Mean Assumption

To test the Zero Conditional Mean (Exogeneity) assumption, we perform a residual analysis. This involves regressing the residuals from the main regression model on the independent variables. If the coefficients in this regression are statistically significant, it suggests that the residuals are correlated with the predictors, indicating a potential violation of the assumption.

5. Conclusions

5(a) Implications of this model

The model demonstrates that sentence length in Cook County is highly predictable using legally relevant case characteristics such as offense type, charge class, and sentence type, which account for the majority of variation in sentencing outcomes. At the same time, the analysis shows that judicial assignment and defendant demographics remain systematically associated with sentence length even after conditioning on observable case factors. This suggests that sentencing outcomes reflect not only statutory and procedural considerations, but also institutional and demographic patterns that are not fully captured by case-level variables alone.

Importantly, the model’s predictive strength does not imply causal relationships. Rather, it highlights where unexplained variation persists, particularly for longer sentences, which are likely influenced by factors absent from the data such as criminal history, plea negotiations, guideline departures, and case-specific aggravating circumstances. The results therefore underscore both the explanatory power and the limitations of administrative sentencing data.
